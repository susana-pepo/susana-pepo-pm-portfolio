01

Song Discovery

Making the right songs findable — Search, Favorites, and what the data said

User Research A/B Testing Data Analysis

Problem

Skoove had 500+ songs in its catalogue, but users struggled to find what they were looking for. Discovery depended entirely on browsing curated collections — which worked for new users but frustrated returning learners who knew exactly what they wanted to play.

Process

Contributed across discovery, scoping, and post-launch analysis for both the Song Search MVP and the Favorites feature. Coordinated QA across platforms and monitored Mixpanel results after launch. For Favorites, designed unmoderated usability research (via Maze), led KPI tracking, and produced the post-experiment report.

Outcome

Search became the #1 discovery method in the Songs Tab, surpassing suggested songs. Favorites hit its primary KPI: 30% of premium users who visited Songs saved at least one song. The search paywall A/B informed a strategic product decision about free trial access.

02

Scaling the Catalogue

Extending a music library means solving licensing, production, and quality at the same time

Content Strategy Licensing Content Ops

Problem

Skoove’s song catalogue was a growth lever with a ceiling on it. Every new track required licensing coverage, manual production work, and editorial judgment. Content production was manual and time-intensive. Licensing coverage had gaps.

Process

Approached it as three interconnected problems. First, catalogue research using Mixpanel data, user feedback, and competitor benchmarking. Second, extending coverage through our ICE agreement — structured around usage thresholds rather than per-song fees. Third, optimizing the internal CMS (Freddie) to streamline workflows and support bulk creation at scale.

Outcome

A meaningfully expanded catalogue with a more scalable production foundation. Licensing coverage extended via ICE without disproportionate cost impact. Content pipeline improved to support higher-volume output per quarter.

03

Bringing the Hits to the App

The licensing work behind getting Billie Eilish, Bad Bunny, and today’s biggest artists into a music learning app

Negotiation Publisher Relations Stakeholder Mgmt

Problem

Music learners want to play what they’re listening to. But making that happen inside a learning app is a licensing puzzle. Every major artist is covered by a web of publishers, performance rights organizations, and territorial agreements. The cost structure has to work too.

Process

Led the licensing negotiations that unlocked access to contemporary, high-demand repertoire — including the UMPI/SACEM multi-year agreement. Worked through advance amounts and installment schedules, free trial carve-outs, recoupment tracking via BMAT, and multi-market coverage. Closed GMR for US performance rights and secured separate Roland licensing for white-label partnerships.

Outcome

Skoove users can now learn to play songs from artists like Billie Eilish and Bad Bunny. Licensing cost ratio improved from 18% to 14.7% of revenue (Jan 2026). Revenue grew from €236K to €315K (Dec ’25 → Jan ’26).

04

Violin Learning, Now in Japanese

Taking Trala to Japan — from zero infrastructure to a full market entry

International PM Localization Vendor Mgmt

Problem

Trala had no Japanese-language experience — no translated UI, no localized educational content, no dubbing infrastructure. A potential Yamaha partnership made market entry strategically urgent. Everything needed to be built from zero.

Process

Hired and onboarded a 4-person localization team. Designed the three-workstream structure: iOS static strings, educational text, and video dubbing — with separate timelines, QA checkpoints, and dependency mapping. Ran an engineering spike to assess AI dubbing feasibility. Also ran a full audit and cleanup of the iOS Localizable.strings file.

Outcome

Three workstreams running in parallel, on track for MVP delivery (target: Sep–Oct 2026). The project also surfaced a bigger organizational question: what does scalable localization infrastructure look like?

Content at Scale

Centralizing the system that keeps music learning fresh across 6+ languages

Content Ops CMS Process Design

Problem

Running content for a music learning app with 1M+ users means shipping constantly — new songs, seasonal collections, localized versions across 6+ languages — without ever letting quality slip. Content workflows were manual and fragmented.

Process

Worked on three levels: the CMS (centralizing and optimizing Freddie, reducing engineer dependency), the localization pipeline (bulk workflows across 6+ languages with QA coordination), and the content calendar itself (seasonal collections, licensing clearance, App Store timing). Built the Mixpanel → CSV → leadership reporting pipeline for copyrighted content usage.

Outcome

A content operation that can now scale. Bulk creation supported by a centralized CMS. Localization pipeline running across 6+ languages with repeatable QA. Multiple seasonal collections shipped per quarter, on schedule. Content decisions connected to real data.

06

Building the Backbone

The financial model and strategic framework behind Skoove’s licensing operation

Financial Modeling Product Strategy Process Design

Problem

Skoove’s licensing operation had two connected gaps. Strategically, deals had grown organically with no overarching framework. Operationally, the financial model required product and content knowledge that the Finance team didn’t have.

Process

Co-authored the Licensing Strategy 2026 with the CPO, leveraging the ICE agreement’s usage-threshold structure to scale repertoire without proportional cost growth. Took over the financial model from Finance through a structured handover. Built a QA framework with cost-to-revenue ratio monitoring (normal 11–12%, alarm at 20%), customer number validation, and formula audits across 6+ tabs.

Outcome

A licensing operation running on a clear strategic framework and self-sustaining financial model. Licensing cost ratio: 14.7% (down from 18%) in January 2026. Revenue grew from €236K to €315K (Dec ’25 → Jan ’26). ICE catalogue expansion path defined and scoped for 2026.
